Cardiovascular Risk Factor Analysis — Framingham Cohort

Context

This is a self-directed project built to develop and demonstrate applied skills in survival analysis and longitudinal modeling in R, using the classic Framingham Heart Study cohort. The work is organized around two complementary questions: which baseline risk factors predict cardiovascular events, and how key biomarkers evolve over repeated visits.

Survival analysis (time-to-event)

Using the baseline dataset, I identify risk factors — notably smoking and hypertension — associated with the incidence of stroke and coronary heart disease. Kaplan–Meier estimation is used to visualize survival curves across exposure groups, and multivariable Cox proportional-hazards models are used to estimate adjusted hazard ratios. This analysis follows a complete-case approach.

Longitudinal analysis (trajectory modeling)

Using the repeated-measures dataset spanning three visits, I model the evolution of key biomarkers — systolic blood pressure and total cholesterol — over time. Individual trajectories and the population-level trend are visualized with spaghetti plots (geom_line / geom_smooth), and linear mixed-effects models (lmer) are used to account for the within-subject correlation of repeated measurements. Two questions are examined: whether the blood-pressure trajectory differs by sex after adjustment, and whether the cholesterol trajectory differs by smoking status.
